Notes


Of particular interest are the ICT profs, ranked Devapriya > Arpita > Amaan > Rashmi > Kundan > Sidharth > Shamini.

Debayan (FCP) ends the highest ranked by compound score, Madhavi (GB) by holistic assesment, excluding profs with insufficient reviews.

Joost and Gil are within a small margin of each other in almost every metric for LATW, Joost winning cause his attendance policy is better. Saikat is ahead of both (albeit with 4 reviews compared to Joost's 13 and Gil's 23).

STEM profs and courses are perceived as having tougher grading, harder workloads and less interesting material across the board. Although STEM profs are perceived as less engaging than humanities profs, they also have universally laxer attendance policies.

TFs/TAs are viewed very favourably in almost every course, generally being the highest-scoring metric. Maya Saran's and Gautam Menon's TFs are glaring exceptions at 2.2 and 2.47 respectively.

Key


Red indicates that the sample size for the prof is less than 5 reviews and is therefore unreliable. A red shaded entry is NOT a reflection on the prof, just on the data.

engaging: 5 most engaging, 1 least engaging
interesting_material: 5 Most interesting course material, 1 least interesting course material
grading: 5 easiest grading, 1 harshest grading
workload: 5 easiest workload, 1 harshest workload
attendance: 5 laxest attendance policy, 1 harshest attendance policy
TFs: 5 best TFs, 1 worst TFs

holistic: In answer to the question “If you could talk to yourself from three months ago, would you recommend this course overall?
5 indicates the course should’ve been their 1st preference, 1 indicates the course should’ve been their last preference

compound_score: Overall score taking into account all other factors in varying proportions based on their relative importance and reliability

Methodology


I circulated an anonymous survey form among informal networks of whatsapp groups intended for ug24 students enrolled in various foundation courses.

An archive of the form can be found here

Note on reliability


The sample sizes for the responses are extremely small: the largest response set received (Jonathan Gil Harris for Literature and the World) was 23. The lowest was 1. Of a possible review set of 38 courses this sem and an actual review set of 36 courses that were reviewed, 23 data points received >=5 reviews. The rest were excluded from the analysis but included in the full released dataset for transparency reasons. While it’s perfectly possible that this data is still trustworthy (bad faith entries are ~0), student experiences are subjective and there’s really just not enough data so take them with a pinch of salt.

Stay tuned to https://recourseatashoka.netlify.app/ for a more detailed analysis of this and more data before the semester’s over. If you’re interested in contributing, check here. Table was generated with this.
